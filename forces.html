Forces

A force is a push or pull that causes an object to change its motion or shape. Forces are fundamental to understanding the behavior of objects.

Types of Forces

  1. Contact Forces: Forces from physical contact:
    • Frictional Force: Opposes motion between two surfaces in contact.
    • Normal Force: Perpendicular to the surface, exerted by a surface on an object.
    • Tension Force: Exerted by a string or rope when pulled.
    • Applied Force: A force applied by a person or another object.
  2. Non-Contact Forces: Forces without physical contact:
    • Gravitational Force: Attraction between objects with mass.
    • Electromagnetic Force: Forces between charged particles or magnetic fields.
    • Nuclear Forces: Forces holding protons and neutrons in a nucleus.

Characteristics of Forces

  • Magnitude: Measured in Newtons (N).
  • Direction: The direction of application.
  • Point of Application: Where the force is applied.

Newton’s Laws of Motion (Force Relationship)

  • First Law (Inertia): An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion unless acted upon by an external force.
  • Second Law: F = ma → Net force equals mass × acceleration.
  • Third Law: Every action has an equal and opposite reaction.

Force Diagrams

Force diagrams (free-body diagrams) are used to visualize and analyze forces acting on an object. They help determine net force and predict motion.

Applications of Forces

  • Mechanics: Design and analysis of mechanical systems.
  • Engineering: Structures, bridges, and machines depend on force analysis.
  • Physics: Forces explain the motion of particles and celestial bodies.

Real-World Examples

  • Friction: Walking, driving, gripping objects.
  • Gravity: Keeps objects grounded, governs planetary motion.
  • Tension: Found in ropes, strings, and cables.

Worked Examples on Forces

Example 1: Object on a Horizontal Surface

Problem: A 10 kg box is pushed with a force of 20 N on a horizontal surface. If the coefficient of kinetic friction is 0.1, find the acceleration of the box.

Step 1: Frictional force f = μN = 0.1 × 10 × 9.8 = 9.8 N

Step 2: Net force Fnet = Fapplied - f = 20 - 9.8 = 10.2 N

Step 3: Acceleration a = Fnet / m = 10.2 / 10 = 1.02 m/s²

Answer: The acceleration is 1.02 m/s².

Example 2: Object on an Inclined Plane

Problem: A 5 kg block is on an inclined plane at 30°. Find the force required to pull it up with an acceleration of 2 m/s² (frictionless).

Step 1: Parallel weight = mg sinθ = 5 × 9.8 × 0.5 = 24.5 N

Step 2: Net force F = ma + mg sinθ = (5 × 2) + 24.5 = 34.5 N

Answer: Required force is 34.5 N.

Example 3: Tension in a Rope

Problem: A 20 kg block is lifted vertically with an acceleration of 1.5 m/s². Find the tension in the rope.

Step 1: Weight = mg = 20 × 9.8 = 196 N

Step 2: Net force = ma = 20 × 1.5 = 30 N

Step 3: Tension T = mg + ma = 196 + 30 = 226 N

Answer: The tension is 226 N.
