Newton’s Laws of Motion

Newton’s Laws of Motion describe the relationship between a body and the forces acting on it.

History of Newton’s Laws

Sir Isaac Newton formulated his three laws of motion, published in his 1687 work, Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica. These laws describe the relationship between the motion of an object and the forces acting upon it.

1. Newton’s First Law (Law of Inertia)

  • An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion with the same speed and direction unless acted upon by an unbalanced force.
  • Influenced by Galileo Galilei and René Descartes, who explored inertia.
  • Newton formalized these ideas, defining inertia precisely.

Example: A 5 kg block is at rest on a frictionless surface. What force is required to keep it at rest? What force is required to accelerate it at 2 m/s²?

Solution: To keep the block at rest, the net force is zero (F = 0). To accelerate at 2 m/s², F = ma = 5 kg × 2 m/s² = 10 N.

2. Newton’s Second Law

  • The net force on an object equals the rate of change of its momentum, or F = ma (force = mass × acceleration).
  • Provides a quantitative relationship between force, mass, and acceleration.
  • Built upon Newton’s observations and experiments.

Example: A 10 kg object is accelerated at 3 m/s² by a 30 N force. What force is required to accelerate it at 5 m/s²?

Solution: F = ma. Initially, F = 30 N, m = 10 kg, a = 3 m/s². For 5 m/s², F = 10 kg × 5 m/s² = 50 N.

3. Newton’s Third Law

  • For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.
  • Forces act in pairs, with the second object exerting an equal and opposite force.
  • Explains interactions like propulsion and collisions.

Example: A person pushes a wall with 20 N. What force does the wall exert on the person?

Solution: Per Newton’s Third Law, the wall exerts an equal and opposite force of 20 N on the person.
